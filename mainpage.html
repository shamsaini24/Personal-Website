Waffle House Website - Waffle House Restaurant
The Waffle House Restaurant website was a personal project of mine commissioned by one of my friends for his parents' restaurant. What went into the website was the culmination of my high school web dev knowledge and what I had learned during my first term web dev course at BCIT. The website was built using one of my previous projects as a framework for a mobile friendly website, the Marina Cafe. As for languages, pure CSS and Bootstrap were used alongside HTML to build the website, with a bit of Javascript sprinkled in. The website was sucessfully launched on Dec 8, 2018, and was hosted on his personal FTP server. He and his family enjoyed the website, and I ocassionally update the website to improve it. I learned quite a bit during this project. I learned how to discuss details with clients that may not be as informed or concerned about the technical aspects. I also gained some knowledge on web hosting and domain management technologies.

BCIT Hackathon
During the short break between my first and second terms at BCIT, all 50 co-op students were invited to the first annual BCIT Hackathon. We were organized into randomly selected teams of 4 or 5 people and were instructed to create "something a BCIT student would find helpful" within 48 hours. None of us knew how to make a coffee machine, so we created the Productivity Analyzer. The Productivity Analyzer is a web app that asks the user to rank their productivity on a scale from 1 to 10 after a set amount of time, tracking your scores, and visualizing your scores over a certain time period. The app was built using HTML and Javascript for the front-end, SQL for back-end storage, and PHP connecting the two. Unfortunately, due to time constraints and lack of hackathon experience from a majority of our group, we were unable to completely flesh out the features that we intended to implement. However, we learned a lot about working in an Agile/Scrum workflow, as well as gaining experience working with people we have never worked with before. What I learned over those two days has helped me both in school and in the workplace.

Chess Game
During my 2nd term at BCIT, we were tasked to make a functioning Chess Game using the 4 principles of object oriented programming (OOP). This was done over the span of 3 months, individually, using Java in an attempt to make us understand the importance of designing before coding. We were to build the game in stages: (1) design the game, (2) build the board, (3) place the pieces on the board, (4) make the pieces move, (5) make the pieces interact with each other, and (6) add the rules. Designing the UML diagram seemed easy at first, until I had to apply what I had designed. After some design revisions, the board was made. Each step after the first had its fair share of technical difficulty, but a majority of my pain came from my design. Once the rules had been added, and the pieces moved how they should, our teacher had another task for us; converting the game into 3D chess. My design restricted me from altering the board without having the game break, this is when I learned the importance of design. At the end, I got my game to work but had I been more thorough in my design I could have saved myself from a couple long nights.
